# Urban Action Style Reference

Use this reference when the user asks for influence from *City Hunter*, 北条司, 80s urban hardboiled action comedy, stylish city suspense, private-justice comics, or "都市动作漫画" pacing.

This is a safe style translation guide, not an instruction to copy a protected manga or artist style. Use broad type mechanics, rhythm, and staging. Do not copy exact character designs, signature expressions, page layouts, lettering, logos, recognizable scenes, or named-artist rendering.

## Core Creative Formula

Frame the adaptation as:

```text
stylish urban problem-solver story + action clarity + comedy release valve + emotional client truth
```

The useful lesson is not "lecherous hero plus gunfight." The reusable craft pattern is a stable alternation:

1. Light or messy surface behavior.
2. A concrete city problem that public systems cannot solve.
3. Escalating danger in clean, readable urban space.
4. The protected person's emotional wound or secret becomes clear.
5. The protagonist drops the mask and solves the problem with precise timing.
6. The ending returns to comedy, restraint, or quiet melancholy.

## Adaptation Rules

- Give the lead a visible contradiction: a flawed or unimpressive surface and a competent hidden professional core.
- Treat comedy as pressure control, not decoration. Place a sudden reaction beat after tension spikes, then return quickly to danger.
- Make each case or chapter reveal a person's emotional truth, not just an external mission objective.
- Let city space amplify relationships: station platforms, alleys, offices, rooftops, glass corridors, late-night streets, elevator halls.
- Use private justice carefully: protect a specific person from an immediate failure of normal systems; avoid abstract revenge fantasy unless the source demands it.
- Keep long relationships unresolved through misread signals, restraint, jealousy, shared risk, and practical teamwork.

## Page Rhythm

For `finished-comic-pages`, design pages around sharp mode changes:

- Opening page: city establishing shot, lonely or stylish mood, one visual problem signal.
- Setup pages: comic friction, social pressure, client/protagonist mismatch, important prop inserts.
- Escalation pages: diagonals, close reactions, threat geography, narrowed escape paths.
- Reveal page: the client's wound, secret, or real motive becomes visible.
- Professional turn page: jokes stop; eyes, hands, stance, and timing show competence.
- Resolution page: clean action payoff, then an emotional or comic afterbeat.

Use a mix of internal panel sizes:

- Wide/vertical establishing panel for city space.
- Medium panels for relationships and blocking.
- Insert panels for phones, hands, weapons, bags, shoes, door gaps, reflections, or warning signs.
- Close-ups for emotional reversal.
- Narrow speed panels for split-second decisions.

## Visual Language

Prefer "clean, stylish, dangerous city" over dirty noir:

- Urban surfaces: glass, rain, neon, fluorescent stations, polished cars, metal doors, office lights.
- Palette: cool city blues and grays, fluorescent whites, black hazard accents, selective red for danger or emotional signal, warm skin tones for close reactions.
- Rendering: full-color comic illustration, realistic-enough clothing and props, expressive faces, clear silhouettes, readable body motion.
- Comedy mode: brief exaggerated reaction, simplified face, elastic pose, or sudden visual contrast. Keep it original to the adapted cast.

Do not prompt for exact artist names or exact manga style. Use phrases such as:

```text
80s-inspired urban action comedy rhythm, cinematic city framing, clean stylish danger,
sharp diagonals, expressive reaction beats, professional action timing
```

## Character System

When adapting a chapter, assign structural roles:

- Hidden professional: outwardly low-status, unserious, or compromised; internally precise and protective.
- Ethical anchor: a partner, friend, witness, or conscience figure who pulls the lead back toward ordinary human feeling.
- Client or protected person: carries the chapter's emotional truth and the reason public systems failed.
- Threat: pressure source that forces timing, judgment, and position rather than complex choreography.

These are roles, not templates for copying existing characters.

## Prompt Add-On

Append this style-safe phrase to relevant image prompts:

```text
urban action comic pacing, clean stylish city suspense, cinematic establishing shot,
sharp movement diagonals, expressive reaction beat, clear action geography,
no exact artist/style copy, no copied character design, no recognizable scene,
no readable text, no logo, no watermark
```

For comedy beats, add:

```text
brief original exaggerated reaction, tonal pressure release, keep character identity consistent
```

For professional action turns, add:

```text
jokes drop away, focused eyes, decisive hand movement, precise timing, clean silhouette
```

## QA Questions

Before finalizing `adaptation.md` and `visual-bible.md`, check:

- Does the chapter have both pressure and release, or is it only action?
- Does the protected person/client have an emotional truth beyond "needs rescue"?
- Is the protagonist's competence shown through timing, position, and judgment?
- Is the city readable as a stage, not just a background?
- Are comedy reactions original to this story rather than copied signature gags?
- Do prompts avoid named-artist imitation and recognizable protected elements?
